# Presenter Notes

## Slide 2 - What is in it for you?

**Objective of this slide:** show that AI only creates real value for us when we redesign workflows, not just type faster.

Title might sound a complicated. A more easy term is "Vibe coding"

A lot is being discussed about AI. Yes, it's related to privacy-issues, to environment problems, big tech power. All very valid arguments, which we can discuss. In this presentation, I'll focus on Vibe coding for increased productivity.
In research it is shown that teams using AI assistance can achieve clear productivity gains. This ofcourse very much depends on the sector. A well-known field study in customer support reported roughly 15% higher productivity. We are not a support desk, but the underlying principle still applies: recurring, knowledge-intensive tasks can often be accelerated.

An important risk is that task-level gains in a team, do not automatically become whole organisation-level gains. These new workflows require good process design. Because extra output can create also more review burden, inconsistencies, or additional quality-control work.

In this presentation, I want to show how to avoid these pitfalls. Start small and measurable. Select one or two high-volume processes, for example report preparation, standard data-cleaning tasks, or an e-learning architecture.
When planning vibe coding, time for the following factors have to be taken into consideration:

- Importantly, preparation time,
- Be aware of a certain error rate,
- Take time for reviewing and fact-checking,
- and importantly, reproducibility.

Only implement AI, scale-up and improve without increasing risk. This way, we keep innovation and reliability in balance.

**Speaking time:** about 3 minutes.

## Slide 3 - AI discussion in context

**Objective of this slide:** present a realistic view of where we currently stand with AI in public and regulated organisations.

Where are we now with AI in organisations like RIVM? The key point is that technology is progressing faster than organisations can absorb it. Many reports show broad AI use, but slower progress in structural integration into processes, governance, and quality assurance. That is exactly the tension we also recognise at RIVM.

For colleagues in microbiology and infectious disease control, this means: feel free to experiment, but make a clear distinction between experimentation and production. A proof of concept with attractive output is not yet an operationally reliable process.

Practically, we can address this by working in two tracks:

- track 1: innovation and exploration, with room to learn quickly,
- track 2: production and decision-making, with explicit requirements for validation, logging, and traceability.

This prevents ad hoc AI use from unintentionally influencing formal analyses or reporting, while still keeping our learning speed high.

**Speaking time:** about 2 minutes.

## Slide 4 - Why Vibe coding?

**Objective of this slide:** explain why this moment differs from earlier technology hypes, and why low-barrier experimentation with code and automation is especially relevant now.

A personal story from myself. During my PhD, I worked with SPSS, a strong but closed package. Only later, when I learned R, did I see how much freedom you gain i could get when I can adapt tooling and workflows myself.

With coding agents and tools such as GitHub Copilot, I, or we,  are at a similar point again. The gain is not only speed but also accessibility: we can build prototypes faster, and test ideas without immediate dependence on expensive or rigid software stacks.

However, there is also a clear boundary. Literature shows these systems perform best within clearly defined task boundaries and explicit checks. Outside those boundaries, quality can decline quickly. So the lesson for RIVM is NOT: let AI code freely. The lesson is: use AI for verifiable steps, with human review and a clear definition of what is good enough.

That makes vibe coding interesting, not as a replacement for expertise, but as an accelerator and improvement to work within your domain.

**Speaking time:** about 2 minutes.

## Slide 8 - What else can we do?

**Objective of this slide:** show practical next-step applications beyond this demo, while keeping expectations realistic.

This is where I want to broaden the picture. The value is not limited to coding tasks. It's up to you to let your creative mind flow and imagine new applications.

The opportunity is speed and consistency, as long we set up clear templates and quality checks. We should stay critical: research suggests that as AI becomes normal in daily work, review quality can decline unless accountability and review processes are explicitly maintained. Furthermore, LLMs (or AI) tend to reflect more mainstream ideas unless otherwise directed (Andersonet al. 2024). Alternative approaches to Vibe-coding can result in more creative responses

My practical recommendation is to start with one pilot workflow where the baseline is clear: how long it takes now, what quality level is required, and who is the final responsible person. Then run AI-assisted and manual processing in parallel for a limited period. If quality is stable and time improves, we can expand in a controlled way.

**Speaking time:** about 2 minutes.
